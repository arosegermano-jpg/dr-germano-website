Your most common concerns about joint pain, treatment, and recovery—explained simply.

Top Questions Patients Ask

What conditions do you treat as an orthopedic surgeon? +

As an orthopedic surgeon specializing in hip and knee conditions, Dr. Germano sees patients for arthritis, cartilage wear, meniscus injuries, ligament problems, joint deformity, and pain after previous surgery. He also evaluates patients with hip or knee pain that affects walking, sleep, work, or sports, even if they are not yet candidates for surgery.

Do I need a referral, or can I make an appointment directly? +

In many cases, patients can call the office and schedule directly. Some insurance plans, however, require a referral from your primary care physician or another provider. If you are unsure, we recommend contacting your insurance company or calling the office so our team can help you understand what is required.

What should I bring to my first appointment? +

Please bring a photo ID, insurance card, a list of your current medications, and any recent imaging studies (X-rays, MRIs, or CT scans) related to your hip or knee. If you’ve had prior surgery, operative reports or previous records can be very helpful. Wearing loose, comfortable clothing makes it easier to examine the joint.

How do I know when it’s time to consider joint replacement? +

Joint replacement is usually considered when arthritis or joint damage causes pain that no longer responds well to medications, injections, physical therapy, or activity modification. If pain limits your ability to walk, climb stairs, sleep, or enjoy the activities you love, it may be time to discuss whether hip or knee replacement is appropriate for you.

What is robotic or computer-assisted hip and knee replacement? +

Robotic and computer-assisted techniques use advanced imaging and planning software to help the surgeon position implants with a high degree of accuracy. The robot does not perform the surgery on its own; rather, it is a tool that helps the surgeon execute a precise, patient-specific plan based on your anatomy.

How long is the recovery after hip or knee replacement? +

Recovery varies from patient to patient. Many people walk with a walker or cane within days of surgery and gradually transition to more normal activities over the following weeks. Most patients make steady progress for three to six months, and the full recovery can continue to improve for up to a year. Your exact timeline depends on your health, activity level, and the type of procedure performed.

Will I need physical therapy after surgery? +

Yes, physical therapy is a key part of recovery for most patients. Early on, therapy focuses on safe walking, swelling control, and gentle motion. As you progress, strengthening and balance exercises help you return to everyday activities. Dr. Germano will provide a specific therapy plan tailored to your surgery and goals.

What are the risks or possible complications of joint replacement? +

Joint replacement is a very successful procedure for many patients, but like all surgery, it carries risks. These can include infection, blood clots, stiffness, dislocation, leg length differences, nerve or blood vessel injury, and wear or loosening of the implants over time. Before surgery, Dr. Germano will review your personal risk factors and the steps taken to reduce those risks.

How long will my hip or knee replacement last? +

Modern joint replacements are designed to be durable. Many implants can last 15–20 years or longer, especially with newer materials and improved techniques. However, longevity depends on factors such as your age, activity level, weight, bone quality, and overall health. Regular follow-up visits allow your surgeon to monitor the implant over time.

How can I contact the office if I have questions after my visit or surgery? +

If you have questions after your appointment or surgery, please call the office using the phone numbers listed on the Locations page. For urgent issues after hours, the on-call provider can be reached through the main office number. In an emergency, always call your local emergency number or go directly to the nearest emergency room.
