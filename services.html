Services

Specialized treatment options designed to restore mobility and relieve pain.

Total Hip Replacement

What is it? Total hip replacement is a surgical procedure that removes the damaged parts of the hip joint and replaces them with smooth, durable implants. The goal is to relieve pain, restore motion, and allow patients to return to normal daily activities with improved comfort and stability.

What to expect: Before surgery, you’ll meet with Dr. Germano to review your symptoms, imaging, and overall health. On the day of surgery, the arthritic portions of the ball-and-socket joint are removed and replaced with a metal stem, a ball, and a socket liner designed to move smoothly together. Most patients begin walking with assistance the same day or the morning after surgery and continue recovery with a structured rehabilitation plan.

Benefits of surgery: For many patients, hip replacement significantly reduces or eliminates joint pain, improves walking and mobility, and allows a return to activities that were limited by arthritis. Modern implant designs and surgical techniques are focused on long-term durability and a more natural feeling hip.

Who is a good candidate?

  • Persistent hip pain that limits walking, sleep, or daily activities
  • Advanced arthritis or joint damage seen on X-rays
  • Pain that has not improved with medications, injections, or physical therapy
  • Difficulty performing routine tasks such as putting on shoes or climbing stairs

Total Knee Replacement

What is it? Total knee replacement is a procedure that resurfaces the worn ends of the thigh bone, shin bone, and sometimes the kneecap with smooth, contoured implants. These components are designed to glide together comfortably, reducing pain and improving knee function.

What to expect: During surgery, the damaged cartilage and a thin layer of bone are carefully shaped and replaced with metal and plastic components that match your anatomy. After surgery, most patients begin standing and walking with assistance soon after arriving in the recovery area. A focused physical therapy program helps restore strength, motion, and confidence in the new joint over the weeks that follow.

Benefits of surgery: Total knee replacement is intended to relieve arthritis pain, improve stability, and allow smoother bending and straightening of the knee. Many patients experience improved walking tolerance, better sleep, and a return to activities that were previously limited by knee pain.

Who is a good candidate?

  • Severe knee pain from arthritis affecting most or all of the joint
  • Stiffness or loss of motion that interferes with daily activities
  • Pain that persists despite medications, injections, or physical therapy
  • Difficulty walking, climbing stairs, or standing for longer periods

Partial Knee Replacement

What is it? Partial knee replacement is a procedure that treats arthritis limited to one area of the knee, most often the inner (medial) compartment. Instead of resurfacing the entire joint, only the damaged portion is replaced with a small implant, preserving healthy bone, cartilage, and ligaments.

What to expect: During surgery, the worn surfaces in the affected compartment are carefully removed and replaced with a contoured metal and plastic implant. The remainder of the knee is left intact. Many patients go home the same day or after a short stay and begin walking with assistance shortly after surgery. Physical therapy focuses on restoring motion, strength, and a smooth, natural gait.

Benefits of surgery: Because only part of the knee is replaced, patients often experience a smaller incision, less discomfort in the early recovery period, and a knee that can feel more “natural” compared with a full replacement. Many patients return more quickly to daily activities and low-impact exercise.

Who is a good candidate?

  • Arthritis confined to a single compartment of the knee
  • Good range of motion and stable ligaments
  • Knee pain that persists despite medications, injections, or therapy
  • Desire to preserve as much of the natural knee as possible

Revision Hip & Knee Surgery

What is it? Revision surgery is a specialized procedure performed when a previous hip or knee replacement is no longer functioning as it should. The operation may involve removing and replacing worn, loose, or damaged components, addressing bone loss, or correcting instability around the joint.

What to expect: Revision procedures are often more complex than first-time joint replacements and require careful planning. Before surgery, Dr. Germano reviews prior records, X-rays, and advanced imaging to understand why the joint is failing. During surgery, old components and scar tissue are removed, the bone is carefully reconstructed when needed, and new implants are placed to restore alignment and stability. Recovery may be slower than with an initial replacement, and a structured rehabilitation program is important.

Benefits of surgery: The goal of revision surgery is to relieve pain, improve function, and restore confidence in the joint. Many patients experience improved stability, better alignment, and the ability to return to daily activities that had become difficult or unsafe.

Who is a good candidate?

  • Ongoing pain in a previously replaced hip or knee
  • Loose, worn, or broken implants seen on imaging
  • Recurrent dislocations, giving way, or instability
  • Failure of prior surgery due to infection or fracture (after appropriate medical treatment)

Robotic-Assisted Surgery

What is it? Robotic-assisted surgery uses advanced 3D imaging and computer guidance to help plan and perform hip and knee replacements with a high degree of precision. The robot does not replace the surgeon; instead, it provides real-time feedback and helps fine-tune implant positioning based on each patient’s anatomy.

What to expect: Before surgery, detailed imaging is used to create a virtual model of your joint and plan the ideal size and placement of the implants. In the operating room, Dr. Germano uses robotic guidance to prepare the bone and position the components according to this plan, making adjustments as needed based on how the joint moves during surgery. Recovery follows the same general pattern as a traditional replacement but may benefit from more precise alignment and soft-tissue balance.

Benefits of surgery: Robotic assistance is designed to support accurate implant placement, more consistent alignment, and personalized joint balancing. These factors may contribute to improved function, stability, and satisfaction for many patients.

Who is a good candidate?

  • Patients undergoing hip, total knee, or partial knee replacement
  • Individuals who want a highly customized, technology-assisted approach
  • Patients with complex anatomy where precision is especially important

AI-Assisted Planning

What is it? AI-assisted planning uses computer algorithms to analyze imaging and alignment data to help optimize how implants are positioned for hip and knee replacement. These tools can simulate different implant sizes and positions to support decisions that best match the patient’s anatomy and goals.

What to expect: Before surgery, your imaging studies are reviewed with the assistance of specialized software that models how your joint will move with different implant options. This information helps Dr. Germano refine the surgical plan in advance. During surgery, the plan is used alongside robotic or computer guidance to carry out the operation as precisely as possible.

Benefits of surgery: AI-assisted planning is intended to support more consistent implant positioning, improved alignment, and a personalized approach to reconstruction. For patients, this may translate into a joint that feels more natural and functions more reliably in daily life.

Who is a good candidate?

  • Patients undergoing hip or knee replacement who want a highly individualized plan
  • Individuals with complex alignment or prior surgery around the joint
  • Patients interested in the use of advanced technology to support surgical decision-making

Hip Arthroscopy

What is it? Hip arthroscopy is a minimally invasive procedure performed through small incisions using a camera and specialized instruments. It is used to treat certain conditions inside the hip joint, such as labral tears, femoroacetabular impingement (FAI), and areas of cartilage damage.

What to expect: During surgery, a small camera is inserted into the hip joint to allow a clear view of the cartilage, labrum, and bone surfaces. Through separate small incisions, instruments are used to repair or remove damaged tissue and to gently reshape areas of bone that are causing impingement. Most patients go home the same day. Crutches are often used for a short period, followed by guided physical therapy to restore motion and strength.

Benefits of surgery: For appropriately selected patients, hip arthroscopy can reduce pain, improve hip motion, and may help delay or prevent progression of damage in the joint. The smaller incisions and minimally invasive approach typically allow for a quicker early recovery compared with open surgery.

Who is a good candidate?

  • Hip pain related to labral tears or femoroacetabular impingement (FAI)
  • Symptoms that have not improved with rest, activity modification, or therapy
  • Limited arthritis on imaging, with most joint surfaces still preserved

PRP & Biological Injections

What is it? Platelet-rich plasma (PRP) and other biological injections use components from your own blood or specialized injectable medications to help reduce inflammation and support the body’s natural healing response. These treatments may be used for certain tendon problems or early joint arthritis when surgery is not yet needed.

What to expect: For PRP, a small amount of blood is drawn from your arm and processed to concentrate the platelets. The PRP is then carefully injected into the targeted area, often using imaging guidance for accuracy. After the injection, mild soreness is common for a short period, and activity is gradually increased as tolerated. Other types of injections, such as cortisone or hyaluronic acid (“gel” injections), may also be recommended based on your specific condition.

Benefits of treatment: Injections can help decrease pain, improve function, and delay the need for surgery in some patients. They are typically performed in an office or procedure setting and do not require anesthesia or an overnight stay.

Who is a good candidate?

  • Mild to moderate arthritis of the hip or knee
  • Tendon or soft-tissue irritation that has not fully responded to rest or therapy
  • Patients seeking nonsurgical options before considering joint replacement
